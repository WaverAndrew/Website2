The Shape
of an Argument

How a piece of writing earns the reader's next sentence — and the one after that.

by Your Name

This is the standfirst — the paragraph that sits under the title and tells the reader what they're in for. Keep it to three or four sentences. Everything below the fold is set in the reading column to the right of the timeline rail.

    I

    Where to begin

    The first paragraph carries more weight than any other. It decides whether the second paragraph gets read at all. So spend it.

    Each section below is a <section class="chapter"> with a data-chapter attribute and a data-label. The label is what shows up as a dot on the left rail, and the rail tracks your scroll position automatically. Add or remove sections and the rail rebuilds itself — no manual table of contents to maintain.

    Pull-quotes, dialogue, and asides go in a blockquote. They sit in a tinted card to break the column.

    II

    Holding the middle

    The reading column is capped near 720 pixels — about 70 characters a line — because that's where the eye stops getting tired. Body copy is PT Serif at 18px with generous line-height; section numbers and labels are Geist Mono, uppercase, widely tracked. The contrast between the two is the whole personality of the design.

    A pull-quote with class="pull" gets the accent rule on its left edge — use it for the line you want people to screenshot.

    You can drop in figures, too. Captions are set in mono so they read as apparatus rather than prose.

    III

    Landing it

    End where the argument actually ends, not where you run out of energy. The dark-mode toggle in the header is persisted to localStorage and respects the reader's system preference on first visit.

    To publish a new essay: copy this file, change the title, hero, and the chapter sections, then add a card to index.html. That's the whole workflow.
